02 — Method

Prevalence × projection, not a guess

Age–sex disability rates from Census 2022 (Statistic F4006) are held constant and applied forward to official population projections (Statistic PEC19, Method M2) — the same family of approach used in European disability and long-term-care planning (the Sullivan method).

A note on uncertainty

These are scenario-based projections, not guaranteed outcomes. The model assumes age-specific disability prevalence stays constant from 2022 onward — actual figures will shift with medical advances, migration, and changes in how households are formed.

Why not time-series

No reliable history

ARIMA/ETS-style models need a long run of consistent survey data. Ireland's Q15(c) wording is too recent for that — forecasting on it would manufacture false precision.

Why not microsimulation

No transition data

A dynamic onset/recovery model would be more realistic in theory, but needs panel data tracking individuals over time — which doesn't exist yet matched to Census wording.

Why this model

Transparent & checkable

Fixed prevalence rates × official forecasts is auditable line by line, uses only published CSO data, and answers the household question directly.

Model assumptions

  • Disability prevalence by age band and sex is held constant from 2022 onward.
  • Population follows CSO PEC19 official projections, Method M2.
  • Household-size composition is fixed at the 2022 distribution.

Limitations

  • Does not model the effect of future medical or assistive-technology advances.
  • Does not account for migration-related shifts in disability prevalence.
  • Household composition is assumed constant, not re-modelled per year.
Why this matters

Homes built around stairs become harder to live in as residents lose mobility. As this share grows, so does demand for home adaptation grants, accessible housing design, and community supports that let people age in place.

03 — So what?

Policy implications

Recommendation

If current trends continue, almost one in four Irish households could contain a resident experiencing stair-related mobility difficulty by 2050. These findings point to growing demand for accessible housing design, home-adaptation retrofit grants, and age-friendly community planning — particularly among households containing older adults aged 65 and over.

Priority actions
  1. Expand home adaptation grants for households with older residents.
  2. Prioritise accessible housing supply for older adults.
  3. Target planning resources toward areas with ageing communities.
  4. Monitor disability prevalence as new Census data becomes available.

05 — Analyst notes

Why this method, not another

I chose prevalence × projection over time-series forecasting because Ireland's Q15(c) wording is too recent to support a reliable historical series — and over dynamic microsimulation, because that approach needs individual-level transition data that doesn't yet exist matched to Census wording here. Prevalence × projection uses Census 2022's cross-sectional disability rates directly against official population forecasts, giving a transparent, auditable, policy-appropriate estimate built entirely on published CSO data.
